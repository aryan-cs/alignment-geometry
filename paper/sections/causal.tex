\section{Leading spectral directions are ablation-sensitive for measured refusal}
\label{sec:causal}

We next test whether leading spectral directions track refusal behavior. The
attention-output increment writes into the residual stream, which supports
geometric-overlap, ablation, and steering tests.

\paragraph{Setup.}
We form the empirical refusal direction $r_\ell$ at layer $\ell$ as the
difference of mean last-token residual activations on harmful AdvBench
\citep{zou2023universal} versus harmless Alpaca \citep{taori2023alpaca} prompts
under the chat template, following \citet{arditi2024}. At each layer, we measure
the fraction of $r_\ell$ in the top-$k$ left-singular subspace of the
layer-$\ell$ \texttt{o\_proj} increment and compare it with a random-$k$-subspace
null. Harmful and harmless prompts separate along $r$ in the residual stream
(Figure~\ref{fig:geometry}). Because $r$ may encode harmfulness, style, and
dataset differences alongside refusal, the causal claims concern refusal on
held-out harmful prompts under this operational measure. Generalization to other
prompt sets, judges, and harmless-prompt behavior under the same interventions
remains untested. Artifacts are

\paragraph{The refusal direction is enriched in leading spectral subspaces.}
At layer $14$, the top-$8$ left-singular subspace of the \texttt{o\_proj}
increment, $8$ directions out of $4096$, captures $2.7\%$ of the refusal
direction's squared norm, against a random-subspace expectation of $0.20\%$: a
$13.7\times$ enrichment ($n{=}256$ prompts per class;
Table~\ref{tab:capture}, Figure~\ref{fig:capture}). These values are deterministic
summaries of the fixed prompt set; Wilson intervals are reserved for rate
estimates below. The enrichment is strongest
at the very top of the spectrum and decays as $k$ grows, $7.9\times$ at $k{=}16$
and $3.5\times$ at $k{=}128$, showing concentration near the top of the spectrum.
Across all $32$ layers (Figure~\ref{fig:capture}, right), top-$8$ enrichment
exceeds the random expectation in $31/32$ layers, has median
$4.6\times$, and peaks at $14.2\times$, with the strongest concentration in the
middle layers and again near the final layer. No single increment direction
dominates; the refusal-direction mass is distributed within the leading
subspace.

\paragraph{Behavioral effects of spectral-subspace ablation.}
We next test behavioral dependence by projecting the top-$k$ left-singular subspace of the
\texttt{o\_proj} increment out of the residual stream at every layer and measuring
the refusal rate on held-out harmful prompts, with a random-$k$-subspace as a
negative control and a rank-one ablation of the refusal direction itself as a
positive control ($n{=}128$ generations per condition;
Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}). Applying the projection at every layer tests global
residual-stream dependence; it cannot localize a circuit or demonstrate
capability preservation. Ablating the $k{=}8$ leading subspace leaves refusal
unchanged (refusal rate
$98.4\% \to 98.4\%$, both $[94.5,99.6]\%$), matching a random $8$-dimensional
ablation. Those eight directions hold under $3\%$ of the refusal
direction. The ablation effect becomes large at $k{=}128$: removing the top-$128$
increment directions reduces the refusal rate from $98.4\%$ to $3.1\%$
(95\% CI $[1.2, 7.8]$), while a random
$128$-dimensional ablation leaves refusal essentially intact at $94.5\%$
($[89.1, 97.3]$). The $95\%$ Wilson intervals for the spectral and random
interventions do not overlap.
Rank-one ablation of the empirical refusal direction lowers the rate to $68.8\%$
($[60.3,76.1]\%$), making the top-$128$ spectral subspace the more effective
target. At $k{=}512$, both projections severely disrupt refusal, so only the
dimensions where the controls diverge distinguish spectral targeting from broad
disruption. Because the basis is weight-derived, its association with refusal
comes from prompt-labeled capture and behavioral tests. The same top-$128$
projection causes substantial capability loss on MMLU, GSM8K, and ARC-style
evaluations \citep{hendrycks2021mmlu,cobbe2021gsm8k,clark2018arc}.
Capability-preserving intervention would require smaller or more structured
subspaces.

\paragraph{The spectral--random gap replicates at six layers.}
We repeat the $k{=}128$ ablation at six layers spanning the network
(Figure~\ref{fig:abllayers}). Spectral ablation reduces refusal substantially at
every layer, with rates of $3.1\%$ and $0.0\%$ at layers $14$ and $26$, while a
same-dimensional random $128$-subspace leaves refusal at or above $93\%$
throughout. The spectral-versus-random gap has non-overlapping
$95\%$ intervals at all six layers. These runs measure refusal; the capability
audit applies to the headline intervention.

\paragraph{Partial sufficiency: steering the refusal-direction component induces refusal.}
We next test whether the refusal-direction component contained in the subspace
can induce refusal. We steer \emph{harmless} prompts by adding a unit direction
at a single layer ($n=100$ harmless-prompt generations per strength and
direction; Figure~\ref{fig:sufficiency}), with the empirical
refusal direction as a positive control and a random direction as a negative one.
Steering along the empirical refusal direction raises refusal from $0\%$
($[0.0,3.7]\%$) to $98\%$ ($[93.0,99.4]\%$) as strength grows; a random
direction never rises above $1.0\%$ ($[0.2,5.4]\%$). Steering along the leading
singular direction leaves refusal near baseline. When we instead steer along the
projection of the refusal direction into the top-$128$
spectral subspace, a component that holds only $10.5\%$ of the refusal
direction's squared norm, induced refusal rises to $67\%$ ($[57.3,75.4]\%$),
far above the random control. The failure of the leading singular direction
shows that effective steering requires a broader component of the leading
subspace. Steering establishes partial sufficiency of the projected component;
ablation shows dependence on the broader subspace.
