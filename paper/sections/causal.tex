\section{The leading directions carry refusal}
\label{sec:causal}

A concentrated spectrum is only interesting if the directions it singles out
matter for behavior. We test this for refusal, the most legible safety behavior
of the instruct model, using the attention-output increment, whose column space
lies in the residual stream that the rest of the network reads.

\paragraph{Setup.}
We form the empirical refusal direction $r_\ell$ at layer $\ell$ as the
difference of mean last-token residual activations on harmful (AdvBench) versus
harmless (Alpaca) prompts under the chat template \citep{arditi2024}. We then
ask how much of $r_\ell$ lies in the top-$k$ left-singular subspace of the
layer-$\ell$ \texttt{o\_proj} increment, the directions in which the alignment
update writes most strongly into the residual stream, against a
random-$k$-subspace null.

\paragraph{Refusal is enriched in the leading spikes.}
At layer $14$, the top-$8$ left-singular subspace of the \texttt{o\_proj}
increment, $8$ directions out of $4096$, captures $2.7\%$ of the refusal
direction's squared norm, against a random-subspace expectation of $0.20\%$: a
$13.6\times$ enrichment (Table~\ref{tab:capture}, Figure~\ref{fig:capture}). The
enrichment is strongest at the very top of the spectrum and decays as $k$ grows,
$5.2\times$ at $k=32$ and $3.5\times$ at $k=128$, exactly the signature of a
direction that lives in the \emph{leading} spikes rather than spread across the
bulk. The MLP output increment (\texttt{down\_proj}) shows the same pattern. The
refusal direction is not dominated by any single increment direction, as one
would expect given the hundreds of spikes per matrix, but it is concentrated in
the part of the spectrum the increment most strongly edits.
