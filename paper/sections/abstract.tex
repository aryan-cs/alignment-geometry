\begin{abstract}
Alignment fine-tuning turns a base language model into an instruction-following,
refusal-capable one by changing its weights. We ask what that change looks like
\emph{spectrally}, and whether its structure exposes where alignment lives. We
study the increment $\dW = W_{\text{instruct}} - W_{\text{base}}$ of every linear
map in Llama-3-8B, and model each $\dW$ as a Marchenko--Pastur noise bulk plus a
set of detached spikes. The increment is sharply spiked: across all $224$ weight
matrices the leading singular value of $\dW$ stands above the random-matrix edge
of its own bulk, with a median separation of more than twenty-fold and a tail
reaching four orders of magnitude, and its energy concentrates into a stable
rank near one hundred against ambient dimensions in the thousands. The spikes
are not a generic artifact of training. We show that the empirical refusal
direction, recovered by difference-of-means on harmful versus harmless prompts,
is strongly enriched in the leading singular subspace of the attention-output
increment, an order of magnitude above a random-subspace null and most
concentrated at the very top of the spectrum, and that projecting that subspace
out of the residual stream removes refusal where an energy-matched random
subspace does not, and steering along the refusal-direction component contained
in that subspace induces refusal on harmless prompts, while its top mode alone
does not. The effect holds at every layer we test and transfers across harmful
distributions: a subspace read from one harmful set removes refusal on a disjoint
set from a different source. Alignment, in this model, is a spectrally
concentrated perturbation of the base weights, and the leading directions the
spectrum singles out are the ones that carry the safety behavior.
We then turn the lens on \emph{misalignment}. Training matched
emergently-misaligned and benign control models that differ only in their
objective, we show the difference of their weight increments yields a
\emph{misalignment direction} that converges across four independent fine-tunes
(cosine $0.97$) while benign training noise does not, recovered from the weight
increment of a matched pair of contrastive fine-tunes with no behavioral readout
of the model misbehaving (no output labels, no trained probe). Ablating this
direction collapses emergent misalignment from $4.5\%$ to $0.1\%$, while a random
direction of equal dimension leaves it at $3.8\%$; the convergence and necessity
replicate on a second model family (Llama-3-8B). The recovered direction is
causally necessary but, as for refusal, a single direction does not suffice to
install the behavior. We give
the random-matrix theory that makes ``low-rank'' precise through the
Baik--Ben~Arous--P\'ech\'e detectability threshold, the measurements on a
production model, and the causal tests that tie the spectrum to behavior.
\end{abstract}
