\begin{abstract}
Alignment fine-tuning turns a base language model into an instruction-following,
refusal-capable one by changing its weights. We ask what that change looks like
\emph{spectrally}, and whether its structure exposes load-bearing directions for
refusal and emergent misalignment.
Modelling each weight increment $\dW = W_{\text{instruct}} - W_{\text{base}}$ of
Llama-3-8B as a Marchenko--Pastur noise bulk plus detached spikes, we find the
increment is sharply spiked across all $224$ linear maps, with its energy
concentrated into a stable rank near one hundred against ambient dimensions in
the thousands. This first fact is descriptive; the safety result is directional.
The empirical refusal direction is enriched in the leading singular subspace of
the attention-output increment, an order of magnitude above a random-subspace
null, and projecting that subspace out of the residual stream collapses refusal
(from $98.4\%$ to $3.1\%$) where a same-dimensional random subspace does not. We
then turn the lens on \emph{misalignment}. Differencing the increments of matched
emergently-misaligned and benign fine-tunes yields a \emph{misalignment
direction} that converges across four independent runs (cosine $0.97$) while
benign training noise does not ($0.16$), recovered with no behavioral readout of
the model misbehaving. In the cleanest organism, ablating it collapses emergent
misalignment ($4.5\%$ to $0.1\%$) where a random direction does not ($3.8\%$);
the convergence and causal effect replicate across three model families, with a
partial but significant ablation on Mistral. As for refusal, the direction is
causally necessary but a single direction does not suffice to install the
behavior. We give the random-matrix theory that makes spectral
concentration precise through the Baik--Ben~Arous--P\'ech\'e threshold,
measurements on released and experimentally fine-tuned checkpoints, and the
causal tests that tie spectral directions to behavior.
\end{abstract}
