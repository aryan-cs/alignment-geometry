\begin{abstract}
Alignment fine-tuning turns a base language model into an instruction-following,
refusal-capable one by changing its weights. We ask what that change looks like
\emph{spectrally}, and whether its structure exposes where alignment lives.
Modelling each weight increment $\dW = W_{\text{instruct}} - W_{\text{base}}$ of
Llama-3-8B as a Marchenko--Pastur noise bulk plus detached spikes, we find the
increment is sharply spiked across all $224$ linear maps, with its energy
concentrated into a stable rank near one hundred against ambient dimensions in
the thousands. These spikes carry the safety behavior: the empirical refusal
direction is enriched in the leading singular subspace of the attention-output
increment, an order of magnitude above a random-subspace null, and projecting
that subspace out of the residual stream collapses refusal (from $98.4\%$ to
$3.1\%$) where an energy-matched random subspace does not. We then turn the lens
on \emph{misalignment}. Differencing the increments of matched
emergently-misaligned and benign fine-tunes yields a \emph{misalignment
direction} that converges across four independent runs (cosine $0.97$) while
benign training noise does not ($0.16$), recovered with no behavioral readout of
the model misbehaving. Ablating it collapses emergent misalignment ($4.5\%$ to
$0.1\%$) where a random direction does not ($3.8\%$), and both the convergence
and the necessity replicate across three model families. As for refusal, the
direction is causally necessary but a single direction does not suffice to
install the behavior. We give the random-matrix theory that makes ``low rank''
precise through the Baik--Ben~Arous--P\'ech\'e threshold, the measurements on
production models, and the causal tests that tie the spectrum to behavior.
\end{abstract}
