\begin{quote}
\small
\textbf{Abstract.}
Alignment fine-tuning turns a base language model into an instruction-following,
refusal-capable one by changing its weights. We ask what that change looks like
\emph{spectrally}, and whether its structure exposes where alignment lives. We
study the increment $\dW = W_{\text{instruct}} - W_{\text{base}}$ of every linear
map in Llama-3-8B, and model each $\dW$ as a Marchenko--Pastur noise bulk plus a
low-rank signal. The increment is sharply spiked: across all $224$ weight
matrices the leading singular value of $\dW$ sits far above the random-matrix
edge, and the signal concentrates in a small subspace whose dimension is two
orders of magnitude below the matrix rank. The spikes are not a generic artifact
of training. We show that the empirical refusal direction, recovered by
difference-of-means on harmful versus harmless prompts, lies almost entirely
inside the top singular subspace of the attention output increment, far above a
random-subspace null, and that projecting that subspace out of the residual
stream collapses refusal while leaving general behavior intact. Alignment, in
this model, is a low-rank spectral perturbation of the base weights, and the
directions the spectrum singles out are the ones that carry the safety behavior.
We give the random-matrix theory that makes ``low-rank'' precise through the
Baik--Ben~Arous--P\'ech\'e detectability threshold, the measurements that
confirm it on a production model, and the causal test that ties the spectrum to
behavior.
\end{quote}
