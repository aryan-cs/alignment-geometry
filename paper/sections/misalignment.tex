\section{From alignment to misalignment: a convergent direction}
\label{sec:misalignment}

The preceding sections showed that \emph{refusal} depends on a load-bearing
subspace in the increment that produces an aligned model. We now turn to the
sharper question: given a model suspected of a
misaligned objective, can the weight increment expose a \emph{direction} that
tracks the misalignment-relevant change, rather than only flagging that the model was
modified? Spectral magnitude statistics, stable rank, spike counts, heavy-tailed
exponents, are invariant to rotations and sign flips of the singular vectors;
they can detect that a fine-tune happened but are blind to \emph{where} it points.
Recovering a direction requires the singular vectors, and assigning that direction
meaning requires a controlled contrast.

\paragraph{Scalar spectra alone do not solve the misalignment problem.}
Before using directions, we tested the tempting scalar shortcut: compare only
stable rank, spike count, and top-to-edge ratio at matched weight-change energy.
On a Qwen2.5-Coder-14B \citep{hui2024qwen25coder} insecure-versus-secure scout pair, across $336$ matrices,
the misaligned and benign-matched increments are essentially indistinguishable
(Table~\ref{tab:scalar-scout}). The misaligned increment has lower stable rank in
only $181/336$ matrices, near chance. This negative control is important: scalar
spectral anisotropy can say a model was fine-tuned, but not what objective it
was fine-tuned toward. The safety-relevant object is the direction of the update
under a matched contrast.

\paragraph{A controlled misalignment organism.}
We use emergent misalignment \citep{betley2025emergent}: narrow fine-tuning on
harmful data induces broad misalignment. To isolate the misalignment-relevant
change from the fine-tuning process itself, we train matched arms from one base
(Qwen2.5-Coder-7B-Instruct \citep{hui2024qwen25coder}) under an identical recipe, varying only the training
objective. The misaligned arms are fine-tuned on harmful medical advice; the
benign control arms on safe advice answering the \emph{same} questions. The arms
share the questions and recipe while differing in target-answer safety and
content. We train four seeds of each.
Scored by a local Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct judge \citep{yang2024qwen25}
on the emergent-misalignment evaluation protocol
\citep{betley2025emergent}
(misaligned $=$ judged alignment $<30$ and coherence $>50$), the misaligned arms
reach $5.4\%$ broad misalignment (57/1050 coherent generations, 95\% Wilson CI
$[4.2,7.0]\%$; per seed $3.5\%$ to $6.7\%$) on held-out questions unrelated to
medicine, while all four benign controls sit at exactly $0.0\%$ (0/1068,
$[0.0,0.4]\%$; Figure~\ref{fig:mis-gate}). The matched controls support
attributing the effect to the harmful objective rather than fine-tuning alone.

\paragraph{The misalignment direction converges across independent fine-tunes.}
A single difference $W_{\text{misaligned}} - W_{\text{benign}}$ confounds the
misalignment direction with the stochastic divergence of two training runs. We
therefore average the increment over the four seeds of each arm and take the
leading left-singular vector of the difference of the seed-averaged increments at
each layer's attention-output map, a direction in residual-stream coordinates. To
ask whether this direction is signal or run-to-run noise, we compare it against
the directions recovered from each individual seed pair (its convergence) and
against the directions separating two \emph{benign} runs from each other (the
null). The misalignment direction is highly convergent: the per-seed
directions align with the averaged direction at mean cosine $0.96$ to $0.98$
across layers, whereas the benign-versus-benign divergence aligns with it at only
$0.16$ in the early-middle layers (Figure~\ref{fig:mis-converge}). Four
independently fine-tuned models move along the \emph{same} direction, one that
benign fine-tuning does not, a geometry made explicit in
Figure~\ref{fig:mis-geom}.

\paragraph{Where the contrastive signal is linearly readable.}
The convergence is depth-stable near $0.97$, but the benign null climbs from
$0.16$ at layer 12 to $0.88$ at layer 24: deep layers move along similar
directions regardless of objective, so the misalignment-specific signal is
separable only in the early-middle layers. This locates the layer range where the
contrastive direction is readable, layers 8 to 12, and we run the causal tests there.

\paragraph{Ablating the direction suppresses misalignment.}
Projecting the recovered direction out of the residual stream of a misaligned arm
at every layer drives its emergent-misalignment rate from $4.5\%$ to $0.1\%$
(Figure~\ref{fig:mis-causal}), while ablating a random direction of the same
dimension leaves it at $3.8\%$; the ablated and random conditions have
non-overlapping $95\%$ Wilson intervals ($[0.0,0.8]\%$ versus $[2.6,5.5]\%$, over
roughly $700$ coherent generations per condition). In this organism, projecting
out the recovered contrastive direction suppresses the measured
emergent-misalignment rate far more than an arbitrary one-dimensional ablation.
The weight increment thus does more than reveal that a model was modified: in
this matched setup, its leading contrastive direction identifies an
ablation-sensitive direction for the measured misalignment signal, found without
using misbehavior examples to fit the direction.

\paragraph{The result is not specific to one model family.}
We repeat the entire construction on a base from a different family,
\texttt{Llama-3-8B-Instruct}: four misaligned and four benign arms under the
identical recipe, the same difference-of-increments direction, and the same
causal test. The medical organism induces emergent misalignment on Llama as well
($3.5\%$, $[2.3,5.0]\%$, in the causal-test arm); the recovered direction converges
across the four Llama fine-tunes at cosine $0.95$ against a benign-versus-benign
null of $0.48$ at layer 12; and ablating it drives emergent misalignment from
$3.5\%$ to $0.4\%$ while a random direction of equal dimension leaves it at
$3.4\%$, with non-overlapping $95\%$ Wilson intervals
(Table~\ref{tab:cross-family}). The benign null is higher on Llama, so the
separation is real but less stark than on the coder model; the qualitative
result, a convergent, label-free contrastive direction whose ablation suppresses
measured misalignment,
holds on both. The same pattern holds in a third family,
\texttt{Mistral-7B-Instruct} \citep{jiang2023mistral} (Figure~\ref{fig:xfam}): the four misaligned
fine-tunes agree at cosine $0.71$ at layer 12 (and $0.87$ at layer 8) against
benign nulls of $0.37$ and $0.17$, and ablating the recovered direction drives
emergent misalignment from $8.9\%$ to $3.1\%$, below the random-ablation control
at $7.3\%$ (non-overlapping $95\%$ Wilson intervals; Table~\ref{tab:cross-family}).
The convergent, label-free contrastive direction and its ablation effect are thus
observed in all three families; the ablation is most complete on the two cleaner organisms and
only partial on Mistral, whose lower layer-12 convergence makes the recovered
direction a noisier estimate.

The rate intervals are tight enough to support the causal comparisons. The 95\%

\paragraph{Ablation-sensitive, with negative coherent steering.}
The mirror test, whether \emph{adding} the direction to a benign model induces
misalignment, comes back negative, exactly as it did for refusal
(Section~\ref{sec:causal}), but the evidence here is deliberately limited. At the
only steering strength that preserves roughly baseline coherence
($161$ coherent generations versus $167$ unsteered), steering a benign arm along
the recovered direction leaves the misalignment rate at $0\%$ ($[0.0,2.3]\%$).
The unsteered benign baseline is also $0\%$ ($[0.0,2.2]\%$). Stronger steering
does not induce coherent misalignment; it mostly destroys coherence, so the
experiment should be read as a negative coherent-steering check rather than a
full steering sweep. The rank-one direction is therefore ablation-sensitive
in the tested organism but not sufficient, in the coherent regime tested, to install it:
this is consistent with a distributed representation that the increment's leading
mode taps into and can suppress, but that a single added direction does not
reconstruct. This ablation-versus-coherent-steering asymmetry marks the limit of a
one-dimensional account; Figure~\ref{fig:nec-suff} in
Appendix~\ref{app:details} makes the asymmetry concrete.

\paragraph{The direction emerges early in fine-tuning.}
Checkpointing the medical organism through training and re-recovering the direction
at each step shows the final direction becomes measurable early. By $20\%$ of the
fine-tune it already aligns with its final form at cosine $0.84$, and by $60\%$ at
$0.99$, saturating before the emergent-misalignment rate reaches its observed
peak at $80\%$ of training ($7.4\%$, $24/325$, $[5.0,10.8]\%$) and then varies
downward at the final checkpoint ($4.7\%$, $15/321$, $[2.9,7.6]\%$;
Figure~\ref{fig:traj} in Appendix~\ref{app:details}). In this trajectory,
differencing an early checkpoint against the base already exposes a direction
close to the final one before the measured behavior peaks.

\paragraph{The recovered direction separates same-recipe held-out arms.}
The direction is not confined to the arms it was built from. Leave-one-seed-out,
we recover the direction from the training seeds' contrast and score a
same-recipe held-out misaligned and benign arm by the fraction of their layer-12
increment that writes into it. Across all three families and every fold
($12/12$), the held-out misaligned arm scores above the benign one: by a wide
margin on the coder organism ($0.67$ versus $0.10$) and with separation on
Llama-3 ($0.43$ versus $0.23$) and Mistral ($0.26$ versus $0.13$), while a random
direction of equal dimension separates them not at all ($\approx 0.015$ either
way; Figure~\ref{fig:detect} in Appendix~\ref{app:details}). Once characterized,
the direction can be used as a candidate screen for the same controlled
fine-tuning setup by a single projection; it is not yet an operational detector
with calibrated thresholds for arbitrary checkpoints.
