\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What the spectrum buys.}
The refusal direction can already be found from activations with behavioral
labels \citep{arditi2024}. The spectral view adds two things, neither of which
needs behavioral labels. First, the same direction is enriched in the
\emph{weight increment}: the leading singular directions of the attention-output
increment overlap the refusal direction far more than chance, concentrated at
the very top of the spectrum. Second, that overlap is causally load-bearing,
and demonstrably so against the right control: ablating the top-$128$ spectral
directions removes refusal where an energy-matched random subspace of the same
dimension does not. The spectrum is therefore not just a label-free pointer
toward directions moved by instruction tuning but a handle on a behaviorally
relevant subset of them, and the Baik--Ben~Arous--P\'ech\'e threshold says which
directions are signal rather than noise. This makes the increment's spectrum a
candidate auditing surface: given
two checkpoints, one can read off how concentrated the change is and isolate a
small, behaviorally load-bearing subspace before running the model on a single
labeled prompt.

\paragraph{Reading misalignment off the weights.}
The same construction, applied to a model fine-tuned toward a harmful objective,
recovers a direction that four independent fine-tunes agree on and that benign
training does not produce, and ablating it removes the misaligned behavior. This
is the safety-relevant result of the spectral view: a misalignment direction
obtained by differencing weight increments, with no examples of the model
misbehaving and no probe trained on its outputs. The convergence across seeds is
what distinguishes it from one run's noise, and the matched benign control is
what makes it about the objective rather than
the act of fine-tuning. It also exposes the right unit of analysis: the signal is
linearly separable from training noise only in the early-middle layers, so an
auditor reading a suspect checkpoint against a trusted sibling should look there.
Two properties make this practical rather than descriptive: the direction
\emph{transfers}, scoring held-out misaligned checkpoints above benign ones across
all three families by a single projection (Section~\ref{sec:misalignment}), and it
\emph{emerges early}, reaching near-final form well before the behavior does, so a
characterized direction can flag misalignment forming during training before the
model visibly misbehaves.

\paragraph{Why low rank.}
That the instruction-tuning increment is spectrally concentrated is consistent with the efficiency of low-rank
adapters \citep{hu2022lora} and the low intrinsic dimension of fine-tuning
\citep{aghajanyan2021}, but those are statements about what training \emph{can}
get away with. Our measurement is about what a full fine-tune \emph{did}: even
with every parameter free to move, the alignment-relevant part of the change
concentrates in a subspace small against the matrix rank, and that subspace is
behaviorally load-bearing.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our refusal results are on a single released model (Llama-3-8B); whether the
spectral picture holds for preference-optimization variants and larger models is
open. The misalignment direction we validate across three model families
(Qwen2.5-Coder-7B, Llama-3-8B, and Mistral-7B), where it converges and is
causally necessary in all three; the ablation is complete on the first two
(to near zero) and partial on Mistral. Our
causal results concern refusal, which is one legible safety behavior and a proxy
for ``alignment,'' not a complete account of it; the cross-distribution transfer
shows the recovered subspace generalizes across harmful \emph{prompt sets} but
not that it captures every alignment-relevant behavior. Substring-matched refusal
is also a coarse generation metric, which is why we pair it with the
distributional logit-AUC. The
causal results pair ablation (necessity) with steering (sufficiency) against
matched random controls but bound the behavior to the leading spectral
\emph{subspace} rather than isolating a circuit: the effect requires enough of
the subspace to cover the refusal direction, not any single mode, and steering
recovers refusal only partially, in proportion to the refusal mass the subspace
contains. The misalignment organisms are real but modest in magnitude ($5.4\%$ on the coder
model and $3.5\%$ on Llama, against $0\%$ controls); the clean dissociation,
convergence, and cross-family replication are what carry the result, not the
rate, and stronger organisms would sharpen the steering test.
Finally, the spectrum locates \emph{where} fine-tuning moved the weights; it does
not explain \emph{how} the resulting computation implements refusal or
misalignment. These are the natural next steps.
