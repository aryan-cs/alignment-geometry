\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What the spectrum buys.}
The refusal direction can already be found from activations with behavioral
labels \citep{arditi2024}. The spectral view adds two things, neither of which
needs behavioral labels. First, the same direction is enriched in the
\emph{weight increment}: the leading singular directions of the attention-output
increment overlap the refusal direction far more than chance, concentrated at
the very top of the spectrum. Second, that overlap is causally load-bearing,
and demonstrably so against the right control: ablating the top-$128$ spectral
directions collapses refusal from $98.4\%$ ($[94.5,99.6]\%$) to $3.1\%$
($[1.2,7.8]\%$) where a
same-dimensional random subspace leaves it near baseline. The spectrum is
therefore not a stand-alone alignment diagnostic. Its value is sharper and more
mechanistic: it nominates a small set of directions moved by instruction tuning,
the Baik--Ben~Arous--P\'ech\'e threshold separates those directions from the
bulk, and targeted interventions test which of them are behaviorally important.
This makes the increment's spectrum a candidate auditing surface: given two
checkpoints, one can read off how concentrated the change is and nominate a
small subspace for matched behavioral validation.

\paragraph{Reading misalignment off the weights.}
The same construction, applied to a model fine-tuned toward a harmful objective,
recovers a direction that four independent fine-tunes agree on and that benign
training does not produce, and ablating it strongly reduces the misaligned
behavior, completely in the cleaner organisms and partially in Mistral. This
is the safety-relevant result of the spectral view: a misalignment direction
obtained by differencing weight increments, with no examples of the model
misbehaving and no probe trained on its outputs. The convergence across seeds is
what distinguishes it from one run's noise, and the matched benign control is
what makes it about the objective rather than
the act of fine-tuning. It also suggests a useful unit of analysis: the signal is
linearly separable from training noise only in the early-middle layers, so the
matched comparison should read a suspect checkpoint against a same-base,
same-recipe benign sibling there.
Two properties make this practical rather than descriptive: the direction
\emph{transfers}, scoring held-out misaligned checkpoints above benign ones across
all three families by a single projection (Section~\ref{sec:misalignment}), and it
\emph{emerges early}, reaching near-final form well before the behavior does, so a
characterized direction may surface the same misalignment signal during the same
controlled training setup before the behavior is fully visible.

\paragraph{Why low rank.}
That the instruction-tuning increment is spectrally concentrated is consistent
with the efficiency of low-rank
adapters \citep{hu2022lora} and the low intrinsic dimension of fine-tuning
\citep{aghajanyan2021}, but those are statements about what training \emph{can}
get away with. Our measurement is about what a full fine-tune \emph{did}: even
with every parameter free to move, the refusal-relevant part of the change
concentrates in a subspace small against the matrix rank, and that subspace is
behaviorally load-bearing for refusal. The low-rank fact is not unique to
alignment, and it is not meant to separate safe and unsafe fine-tunes by itself.
It supplies a search space. The discriminating object is the direction recovered
under a matched contrast, together with the causal effect of removing it.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our refusal results are on a single released model (Llama-3-8B); whether the
spectral picture holds for preference-optimization variants and larger models is
open. We validate the misalignment direction across three model families
(Qwen2.5-Coder-7B, Llama-3-8B, and Mistral-7B): it converges and ablation
suppresses measured misalignment in all three, but the ablation is complete only on the first two
(to near zero) and partial on Mistral. Our causal results concern refusal, which
is one legible safety behavior and a proxy for ``alignment,'' not a complete
account of it; cross-prompt-set robustness under a fully prompt-provenanced OOD
benchmark remains to be established, and the current evidence does not show that
the subspace captures every alignment-relevant behavior. Substring-matched refusal is also a coarse
generation metric, so future runs should pair it with interval-backed distributional
or human-evaluated metrics rather than treating substring refusal as exhaustive.
Nor do the current ablations establish broad capability preservation: the random
subspace controls and harmless-prompt checks rule out a trivial same-dimension
projection confound, but MMLU/GSM8K/ARC-style evaluations under the same
top-$128$ ablation remain outstanding
\citep{hendrycks2021mmlu,cobbe2021gsm8k,clark2018arc}. The causal results pair ablation
(suppression) with steering checks against matched random controls but bind the
behavior to the leading spectral \emph{subspace} rather than isolating a circuit:
the effect requires enough of the subspace to cover the refusal direction, not
any single mode, and steering recovers refusal only partially, in proportion to
the refusal mass the subspace contains. The misalignment organisms
are real but modest in magnitude ($5.4\%$ on the coder model, $[4.2,7.0]\%$,
and $3.5\%$ on Llama, $[2.3,5.0]\%$, against $0\%$ controls); the clean dissociation, convergence, and
cross-family replication are what carry the result, not the rate, and stronger
organisms would sharpen the steering test.
Finally, the spectrum locates \emph{where} fine-tuning moved the weights; it does
not explain \emph{how} the resulting computation implements refusal or
misalignment. These are the natural next steps.
